About Eulerian Video Magnification (EVM)

This tool is a browser-based approximation of Eulerian Video Magnification, a technique developed by MIT CSAIL researchers. Standard video captures subtle changes—like the micro-color fluctuations of blood pulsing through skin, or the structural vibration of a motor—that fall below the threshold of human perception. EVM acts as a "microscope for time," isolating specific temporal frequencies and amplifying them to make the invisible visible.

How this web implementation works:

  • Spatial Downsampling: The video is downsampled to a low spatial resolution. This isolates the spatial frequencies we care about and acts as a lightweight alternative to full Gaussian/Laplacian pyramids, making real-time web processing feasible.
  • Temporal IIR Bandpass Filtering: A dual first-order Infinite Impulse Response (IIR) filter evaluates every individual pixel over time. It dynamically adapts to the browser's varying framerate to isolate changes occurring within the chosen frequency bounds (e.g., 0.8–2.5 Hz for a human heartbeat).
  • Recombination: The isolated, amplified temporal differences are upscaled using GPU hardware bilinear interpolation and combined with the original high-resolution frame, preserving overall visual fidelity.
  • Luminance Normalization: Webcams rely on Auto Gain Control (AGC) which causes the entire frame brightness to "breathe". This engine measures and counteracts global luminance drift, preventing webcam auto-exposure from ruining the temporal pixel differences.

For more information on the foundational research, see:
Hao-Yu Wu, Michael Rubinstein, Eugene Shih, John Guttag, Frédo Durand, William Freeman. "Eulerian Video Magnification for Revealing Subtle Changes in the World" (SIGGRAPH 2012).
